## 1. Core Concepts & Frameworks

### 1.1 Degree Heating Weeks (DHW) — NOAA Coral Bleaching Alert Threshold

DHW is the accumulation of thermal stress above the maximum monthly mean (MMM) sea surface temperature for a reef location. Units: °C-weeks.

| DHW Value | NOAA Alert Level | Expected Outcome |
|-----------|-----------------|-----------------|
| 0 | No Alert | No bleaching expected |
| 1–3 | Watch | Bleaching possible |
| 4–7 | Alert Level 1 | Bleaching likely (up to 30% of corals) |
| 8+ | Alert Level 2 | Mass bleaching and mortality likely |
| 16+ | Extreme | Near-total mortality on affected reefs |

Source: NOAA Coral Reef Watch, Eakin et al. (2010). DOI: 10.1371/journal.pone.0013350

### 1.2 Reef Check Methodology

The globally standardized volunteer and scientific reef monitoring protocol. Key parameters:

- **Transect design:** Two 50m × 4m belt transects per site (100m total per survey)
- **Coral parameters:** Live hard coral cover (%), coral genera (minimum 11 indicator genera), bleaching prevalence
- **Fish parameters:** Indicator fish species biomass (butterflyfishes, groupers, humphead wrasse, surgeonfish)
- **Invertebrate parameters:** Crown-of-thorns starfish, diadema urchins, giant clams, lobster, sea cucumbers
- **Substrate parameters:** Rubble, sand, silt, coralline algae, turf algae, macroalgae, soft coral

Source: Reef Check Foundation. Reef Check Training Manual (2020). www.reefcheck.org

### 1.3 GCRMN (Global Coral Reef Monitoring Network) Protocols

The GCRMN provides standardized global monitoring methods that expand on Reef Check for scientific-grade surveys:

- **Photo-quadrat method:** 50cm × 50cm quadrats at 1m intervals along a 50m transect; analyzed via CoralNet or CPCe software
- **Belt transect method:** 50m × 2m belt for benthic cover
- **Underwater video method (UVC):** Continuous video along 25m transect; analyzed by trained scorers
- **Minimum reporting requirements:** Live coral cover %, algae cover %, recent mortality %, bleaching %, rubble %

GCRMN global status report 2020: coral cover ~49% in 1978 → ~17.6% in 2019 across monitored reefs.

Source: Souter et al. (2021). Status of Coral Reefs of the World: 2020. GCRMN. DOI: 10.59387/WOTJ9184

### 1.4 CoralWatch Bleaching Monitoring

Color-based bleaching health index using CoralWatch Coral Health Chart (6-tile spectrum from pale to darkest):

| Score | Color | Health Status |
|-------|-------|--------------|
| 1 | White (fully bleached) | Bleached |
| 2 | Very pale | Severely bleached |
| 3 | Pale | Moderately bleached |
| 4 | Slightly pale | Mildly bleached |
| 5 | Normal color | Healthy |
| 6 | Darkest (symbiont-dense) | Optimal |

Bleaching index = Mean score across all colonies surveyed. Threshold for bleaching alert: mean < 3.

Source: CoralWatch, University of Queensland. coralwatch.org

### 1.5 Ocean Health Index (OHI) — 10-Component Marine Health Framework

Developed by the National Center for Ecological Analysis and Synthesis (NCEAS). Scores 0–100 per component.

| Component | Description |
|-----------|-------------|
| Food provision | Sustainable fisheries + mariculture |
| Artisanal fishing opportunity | Access for local fishers |
| Natural products | Sustainable extraction of non-food products |
| Carbon storage | Capacity of coastal habitats to store carbon |
| Coastal protection | Buffering capacity against storms and erosion |
| Tourism & recreation | Coastal and marine tourism health |
| Coastal livelihoods & economies | Employment and revenue from ocean sectors |
| Sense of place | Iconic species and lasting special places |
| Clean waters | Freedom from pollution |
| Biodiversity | Species and habitats health |

Source: Halpern et al. (2012). An index to assess the health and benefits of the global ocean. Nature, 488, 615–620. DOI: 10.1038/nature11397

### 1.6 Coral Restoration Foundation (CRF) Protocols

CRF (Key Largo, Florida) pioneered large-scale coral nursery and outplanting programs in the Caribbean:

- **Tree nursery method:** PVC pipe "trees" suspended in water column at 4–8m depth; fragments grow 2–4cm/month
- **Fragment size:** Optimal nursery fragment: 2–5cm nubbins from healthy donor colonies
- **Nursery survival rates:** 90%+ under healthy conditions; 70–80% in stressed conditions
- **Outplanting:** Fragments transplanted to reef when 7–15cm length; cement or epoxy attachment
- **Target species:** Acropora cervicornis (staghorn coral), Acropora palmata (elkhorn coral) — both IUCN Critically Endangered
- **Monitoring post-outplanting:** Monthly for 6 months, quarterly thereafter; track survival, growth, bleaching, predation

Source: Coral Restoration Foundation. crf.org; Schopmeyer et al. (2017). DOI: 10.1371/journal.pone.0169966

### 1.7 Micro-Fragmentation (Mote Marine Laboratory)

Pioneered by David Vaughan at Mote Marine Laboratory, Sarasota, FL:

- **Method:** Coral fragments cut to 2–5mm (micro-scale), triggering wound-response rapid growth
- **Growth acceleration:** 25–40× faster than standard fragmentation in some species
- **Species range:** Works on massive/encrusting corals (Orbicella, Diploria, Montastraea) that are difficult to propagate by branching methods
- **Ex-situ application:** Controlled indoor tanks or flow-through seawater systems
- **Limitations:** Requires specialized cutting equipment (tile saw or Dremel); water quality control critical

Source: Forsman et al. (2015). A new method to overgrow massive corals and the role of light in re-epithelialization. PLoS ONE, 10(7). DOI: 10.1371/journal.pone.0133056

### 1.8 SECORE International — Assisted Gene Flow & Sexual Propagation

SECORE focuses on sexual reproduction and genetic diversity in coral restoration:

- **Spawn collection:** Collect coral gametes during mass spawning events
- **Ex-situ fertilization:** Cross-fertilize genetically diverse parent colonies
- **Substrate settlement:** Use specially designed Seeding Units (SU) for larval settlement
- **Assisted gene flow:** Cross-breed thermally tolerant parent colonies to produce more resilient offspring
- **Advantages over fragmentation:** Genetic diversity, no clonal limitations, novel thermal tolerance phenotypes
- **Scale:** 1 spawning event can produce tens of thousands of new recruits

Source: SECORE International. secore.org; Randall et al. (2020). DOI: 10.1007/s00338-020-01893-6

### 1.9 Biorock (Mineral Accretion Technology)

Developed by Tom Goreau and Wolf Hilbertz:

- **Mechanism:** Low-voltage electrical current passed through seawater causes mineral accretion (calcium carbonate + brucite) on steel substrate
- **Benefits:** Increases coral growth rates 2–6×; increases survival during bleaching events; structurally stabilizes substrate
- **Power requirements:** Solar, wind, or grid power; 1.2–12V DC
- **Applications:** Reef restoration in turbid/acidifying environments; breakwater construction
- **Limitations:** Infrastructure cost; maintenance; site-specific effectiveness

Source: Goreau & Hilbertz (2005). Marine ecosystem restoration: costs and benefits for coral reefs. World Resource Review, 17(3), 375–409.

## 3. State-of-the-Art Methods & Tools

### 3.1 Remote Sensing & Satellite Monitoring

- **NOAA Coral Reef Watch (CRW):** Daily 5km resolution SST and DHW maps; bleaching alert email/RSS feed. coralreefwatch.noaa.gov
- **Allen Coral Atlas:** 5m resolution global coral reef mapping using PlanetScope satellite imagery + AI. allencoralatlas.org
- **CoRTAD (Coral Reef Temperature Anomaly Database):** 4km resolution weekly SST since 1982. coris.noaa.gov/data/corals/CoRTAD
- **Landsat/Sentinel-2:** Used for reef extent mapping and change detection at 10–30m resolution
- **Planet Labs SkySat:** Sub-meter resolution for site-specific bleaching visual monitoring

### 3.2 Benthic Image Analysis Software

- **CoralNet:** Cloud-based photo-quadrat annotation platform. Supports machine learning classifier training. coralnet.ucsd.edu
- **CPCe (Coral Point Count with Excel extensions):** Standard point-count analysis for photo-quadrats
- **BenthoBox:** AI-assisted video transect analysis
- **ReefCloud (AIMS):** Automated benthic image classification for Reef Check + GCRMN photo-quadrat data

### 3.3 Water Quality Instrumentation

- **YSI EXO2 Sonde:** Multi-parameter: dissolved oxygen, pH, conductivity, temperature, chlorophyll, turbidity
- **SubCtech OceanPack:** Continuous pCO2 measurement for acidification monitoring
- **SATLANTIC SeaFET:** High-precision pH sensor for reef pH monitoring
- **HOBO Temperature Loggers:** Low-cost autonomous temperature recording (±0.2°C)

### 3.4 Restoration Hardware & Materials

- **PVC pipe tree nurseries:** Low-cost, easy assembly; suitable for fragile branching corals
- **Rope nurseries:** Monofilament lines suspended between stakes; lower snag risk in current
- **Coral Discs (SECORE SU):** Pre-designed limestone settlement substrates for sexual propagation
- **Biorock frames:** Galvanized steel structures for mineral accretion technology
- **ReefBall:** Permeable dome-shaped artificial reef modules for substrate provision

## 5. Analytical Frameworks

### 5.1 Reef Check Assessment Framework

**Applicability:** Any reef site worldwide, trained volunteers or scientists  
**Outputs:** % cover (hard coral, algae, substrate types), fish biomass estimate, invertebrate counts  
**Benchmark:** Compare to regional baselines from Reef Check global database  
**Reference:** Reef Check Training Manual (2020 edition)

### 5.2 GCRMN Standard Protocol

**Applicability:** Scientific monitoring requiring higher precision than Reef Check volunteer surveys  
**Outputs:** Photo-quadrat benthic composition, belt transect fish assemblage, coral demographic data  
**Benchmark:** Compare to GCRMN Status of Coral Reefs of the World (2020) global averages  
**Reference:** GCRMN Methods for Assessing Status and Trends in Coral Reef Communities (2009)

### 5.3 DHW Bleaching Risk Framework (NOAA CRW)

**Applicability:** Any reef globally where NOAA satellite data is available (>99% of reef area)  
**Outputs:** Current DHW value, Alert Level (0 / Watch / Alert 1 / Alert 2), 4-month DHW forecast  
**Decision rule:** DHW ≥ 8 → activate emergency response protocol; DHW ≥ 4 → heightened monitoring  
**Reference:** Liu et al. (2006); NOAA CRW Technical Guidance

### 5.4 CRF Nursery Protocol Scorecard

**Applicability:** In-situ and ex-situ coral nursery projects (primarily branching Acropora spp.)  
**Outputs:** Fragment survival rate (%), monthly growth rate (cm/month), outplanting readiness score  
**Benchmark:** >90% survival = excellent; 70–90% = acceptable; <70% = investigate stressors  
**Reference:** Schopmeyer et al. (2017); CRF Restoration Manual

### 5.5 Statistical Power Framework for Monitoring Design

**Applicability:** Long-term monitoring protocol design  
**Method:** Two-sample t-test power calculation for detecting minimum detectable change (MDC) in coral cover  
**Formula:** n = 2(Zα/2 + Zβ)² × σ² / δ², where σ = SD of coral cover, δ = MDC (typically 5%)  
**Standard:** 80% power (β = 0.20), α = 0.05, two-tailed  
**Reference:** English & Wilkinson (1997). Survey Methods for Tropical Marine Environment. AIMS

## 9. Ocean Acidification Reference Data

| Parameter | Pre-Industrial Value | 2024 Value | Projected 2100 (RCP8.5) |
|-----------|---------------------|------------|--------------------------|
| Surface ocean pH | 8.21 | ~8.08 | ~7.75 |
| pCO2 (ocean surface) | ~280 µatm | ~420 µatm | ~850 µatm |
| Aragonite saturation state (Ωarag) | ~3.5 | ~2.8 | ~1.5 |
| Calcification impact on Acropora | Baseline | −15 to −20% | −50 to −60% |

Aragonite saturation state below 3.0 begins to impair coral calcification; below 1.0 corals dissolve. Most coral reef ecosystems require Ωarag > 2.0 for net positive carbonate budgets.

Source: IPCC SROCC (2019); Orr et al. (2005); Hoegh-Guldberg et al. (2007)
